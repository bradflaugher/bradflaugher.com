Why Competition Between Proprietary Platforms Enables Open Standards

October 19, 2025
A counterintuitive insight: competition between proprietary platforms can promote open standards better than consolidation around the "most open" option. Your strategic choices today determine what's possible tomorrow.

WWDC 2007: The Fork in the Road

A Personal Note

I was in the audience at WWDC 2007 when Steve Jobs announced how third-party apps would work on the iPhone. At the time, many developers were skeptical—they wanted native apps with full hardware access. But looking back nearly two decades later, I still think Jobs was right.

On June 11, 2007, Steve Jobs stood on stage and announced that the iPhone would run third-party applications—but only as web apps. No native apps. No App Store. Just the open web.

In his presentation, Jobs said:

"The full Safari engine is inside of iPhone. And so, you can write amazing Web 2.0 and Ajax apps that look exactly and behave exactly like apps on the iPhone. And these apps can integrate perfectly with iPhone services. They can make a phone call, they can send an email, they can look up a location on Google Maps."

Jobs called this Apple's "sweet solution" for iPhone apps. Apple's official press release stated that web apps could "extend iPhone's capabilities without compromising its reliability or security."

Developer pressure eventually forced Apple to create the App Store in 2008. We chose native apps over web standards. And with that choice, we locked ourselves into two proprietary ecosystems that now control mobile computing.

What if Jobs had been right? What if we'd stayed with web apps?

If the iPhone had remained a web app platform:

  • Developers would have built for open web standards, not proprietary APIs
  • Users could switch between iPhone and Android without losing their apps
  • New platforms could compete immediately—they'd just need a good browser
  • The competitive pressure would have kept web standards vibrant and innovative

Instead, we got app ecosystem lock-in. Jobs' original vision was more strategically aligned with software freedom—even if it was less immediately profitable.

The Counterintuitive Principle

This story illustrates a principle that most people in the free software community get backwards:

Competition between proprietary platforms can promote open standards more effectively than consolidation around a "more open" platform.

The conventional wisdom says: "Always choose the most open platform available. If everyone uses the most open option, we'll collectively move toward freedom."

This sounds logical. It's also strategically naive.

When proprietary platforms compete, they need open standards as neutral ground. When everyone consolidates on one platform—even a "more open" one—those standards die from lack of competitive pressure.

Your strategic choices today determine what's possible tomorrow. Let me show you how this pattern repeats across technology domains.

Mobile Today: The GrapheneOS Trap

The Conventional Wisdom

GrapheneOS appears to be the "most open" mobile option. It's based on AOSP, has better privacy than stock Android, and removes Google services by default. The conventional wisdom says everyone concerned about mobile freedom should use GrapheneOS.

The Problem

But here's what actually happens:

  • GrapheneOS requires Google Pixel hardware. You're literally buying Google's phone.
  • ~90% of users install Google Play Services anyway. Banking, work, and transportation apps don't work without it.
  • It entrenches Android as "the" open mobile platform. Developers target Android APIs exclusively.
  • Progressive Web Apps become irrelevant. Why support web standards if everyone's on Android?
  • Google wins forever. Even in the "free software" world, Google's APIs become the permanent standard.

The Strategic Alternative

Use any phone (iPhone or Android), but reject native apps entirely in favor of Progressive Web Apps.

When iPhone and Android compete, neither can control the standard. Developers who want to reach both platforms need platform-agnostic solutions. The web fills that gap.

By using PWAs exclusively, you:

  • Keep web standards relevant and maintained. Competition drives web API improvements.
  • Remain platform-agnostic. You're not locked into any vendor's ecosystem.
  • Enable future alternatives. When a truly free phone emerges, it just needs a good browser.
  • Vote with your behavior. Every PWA choice promotes open standards.

This is more strategically aligned with software freedom than using GrapheneOS. You're challenging the root problem (closed app ecosystems) rather than accepting a slightly better version of it.

This is exactly what Jobs proposed in 2007. We rejected it then. We can choose it now.

Gaming: Why I Use PlayStation (Not Just Steam Deck)

The Naive View

Steam Deck runs on Linux. Therefore, everyone should use Steam Deck to support Linux gaming. More Steam Deck users means more Linux gaming support. Simple, right?

The Strategic View

Competition between PlayStation, Xbox, and PC gaming forces developers to use cross-platform technologies. When these platforms compete, no single vendor can control the graphics API standard.

Microsoft can't force everyone to use DirectX because Sony and Nintendo need alternatives. This keeps open graphics APIs like Vulkan and OpenGL maintained, relevant, and improving.

Here's what actually happens:

  • Cross-platform development becomes essential. Game studios want titles on all platforms to maximize revenue.
  • Open APIs stay relevant. Engines like Unity and Unreal rely on Vulkan/OpenGL for cross-platform support.
  • Linux gaming benefits indirectly. Work done for PlayStation's graphics stack directly benefits Linux.

The Consolidation Trap

Now imagine everyone consolidated on Steam Deck and Windows PC:

  • DirectX becomes the de facto standard. Why support Vulkan if everyone's on Windows or Proton (which translates DirectX)?
  • Cross-platform engines become less important. Developers target DirectX directly.
  • Open APIs stagnate. Without competitive pressure, Vulkan/OpenGL development slows.
  • Future alternatives become impossible. New platforms must support DirectX, giving Microsoft permanent control.

Paradoxically, using PlayStation (a proprietary platform) maintains the competitive pressure that keeps open graphics APIs alive, which ultimately benefits Linux gaming more than consolidating on Steam Deck would.

The FreeBSD Connection

PlayStation 5's operating system is based on FreeBSD. Sony's investment in PlayStation drives FreeBSD development, with improvements flowing back to the open source BSD ecosystem. If PlayStation didn't exist and everyone used Linux-based systems, this diversity and development investment would disappear. Competition between proprietary platforms built on different open source foundations strengthens the entire ecosystem.

More Historical Precedents

Jobs on DRM (February 2007)

Just months before the iPhone web apps announcement, Jobs published "Thoughts on Music," a rare open letter criticizing Digital Rights Management:

"The third alternative is to abolish DRMs entirely. Imagine a world where every online store sells DRM-free music encoded in open licensable formats. In such a world, any player can play music purchased from any store, and any store can sell music which is playable on all players. This is clearly the best alternative for consumers, and Apple would embrace it in a heartbeat."

Jobs argued that DRM was ineffective and that open standards would benefit everyone. Within a year, iTunes began selling DRM-free music.

Mac's Unix Adoption (2001) Helped Linux

When Apple adopted Unix standards for Mac OS X in 2001, it transformed the Mac's viability for developers. But here's the counterintuitive part: Mac's adoption of Unix standards also helped Linux.

When Mac became Unix-compatible, it validated Unix as a modern desktop platform. Developers built cross-platform tools that worked on Mac, Linux, and BSD. The existence of a commercial Unix desktop (Mac) kept Unix standards relevant and maintained, which benefited the entire ecosystem.

This competitive pressure eventually led Microsoft to create Windows Subsystem for Linux (WSL)—Microsoft couldn't ignore that developers were choosing Mac and Linux because of Unix compatibility. WSL brought Linux directly into Windows, further accelerating Linux adoption everywhere.

Competition between Mac, Linux, and Windows around Unix standards made Unix ubiquitous. If everyone had consolidated on one platform, those standards might have stagnated.

Browser Wars Saved the Web

The same dynamic played out with web standards:

  • Safari, Firefox, and Chrome competed on web standards compliance
  • No single browser could control the web (after IE's dominance ended)
  • Web standards improved rapidly because of competitive pressure
  • This enabled the modern web that works across all platforms

Jobs' original vision for iPhone web apps would have fit perfectly into this ecosystem. The App Store's success came at the cost of platform lock-in—exactly what web standards were designed to prevent.

Technical Examples for the Nerds

Containers: Docker, OCI, and Podman

The container ecosystem provides a perfect technical example. When Docker launched in 2013, it revolutionized application deployment. By 2015, Docker had become so dominant that it risked creating proprietary lock-in.

But then competition emerged, and Docker helped create an open standard to prevent fragmentation.

In June 2015, Docker, CoreOS, and other container industry leaders founded the Open Container Initiative (OCI) under the Linux Foundation. Docker donated its runtime (runc) and image format as the basis for open specifications.

This wasn't altruism—it was strategic recognition that:

  • Multiple container runtimes were emerging (rkt from CoreOS, LXC, others)
  • Kubernetes was gaining traction and needed runtime-agnostic standards
  • Enterprise customers wanted portability across different implementations
  • A standards war would hurt everyone in the ecosystem

What OCI enabled:

  • Podman emerged as a Docker alternative. Red Hat developed Podman as a daemonless, rootless container engine that's fully OCI-compliant. You can run Docker containers with Podman without modification.
  • Kubernetes became runtime-agnostic. Any OCI-compliant runtime works with Kubernetes (containerd, CRI-O, etc.).
  • Container images became portable. Build with Docker, run with Podman, deploy to Kubernetes—all using the same OCI image format.
  • Innovation accelerated. New runtimes like gVisor and Kata Containers could compete on features while maintaining compatibility.

If everyone had consolidated on Docker as "the standard," we'd be locked in. Instead, competition between Docker and Podman maintains pressure to improve the OCI standard itself.

Today, many developers and organizations are switching to Podman for its security benefits (no daemon, rootless containers) while still using Docker images and Dockerfiles. This is only possible because of OCI standards.

AI Agents: MCP and Crush

The AI agent space is experiencing this dynamic right now. Anthropic introduced the Model Context Protocol (MCP), an open standard for connecting AI applications to external systems. Think of it as "USB-C for AI agents."

Here's what's fascinating: MCP exists because of competition between AI providers. If everyone consolidated on a single AI platform (say, OpenAI), that platform would use proprietary APIs for tool integration. There would be no need for an open standard.

But because Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and others compete, developers need a platform-agnostic way to build tools and integrations. MCP fills that gap.

Now we have open source AI agents like Crush by Charm Bracelet that can use MCP to integrate with any tool or data source, regardless of which AI model powers the agent.

What MCP enables:

  • Open source agents are viable. Projects like Crush don't need to build proprietary integrations for every tool.
  • Tool developers write once, support everywhere. An MCP server works with any MCP-compatible agent.
  • No single AI vendor controls the ecosystem. You can switch between Claude, GPT, or local models without losing your tools.
  • Future AI platforms can compete immediately. New entrants just need to support MCP to access the entire ecosystem.

If the AI community had consolidated on "the most open option" (perhaps a single open source model), we likely wouldn't have MCP. The competitive pressure between proprietary AI providers created the need for an open standard, which now benefits everyone—including open source projects.

The General Principle

Competition → Cross-Platform Needs → Open Standards

When proprietary platforms compete, they cannot all use each other's proprietary APIs. This creates demand for open, cross-platform standards. These standards enable future alternatives and prevent any single vendor from achieving permanent control.

When Consolidation Kills Standards

Consolidating on "the most open option" can actually harm open standards when:

  1. It reduces competitive pressure. One dominant platform means no need for cross-platform standards.
  2. It entrenches one vendor's APIs. Even if more "open," those APIs become the de facto standard.
  3. It makes alternatives impossible. Future platforms must be compatible with the dominant platform's APIs.
  4. It reduces diversity. Monocultures are fragile; diversity enables resilience and innovation.

When Competition Promotes Standards

Competition between proprietary platforms promotes open standards when:

  1. No single vendor can control the standard. Multiple platforms need neutral ground.
  2. Cross-platform development becomes valuable. Developers want to reach all platforms.
  3. Open standards fill the gap. They become the bridge between competing platforms.
  4. Future alternatives remain possible. New platforms can use the same open standards.

Strategic Framework for Technology Choices

When Open Solutions Exist: Use Them

For desktop, laptop, and server computing, truly open solutions exist and work well:

  • Hardware: System76, Framework, Purism, or used ThinkPads with coreboot
  • Operating Systems: GNU/Linux distributions (Debian, Fedora, Arch, etc.)
  • Applications: Vast ecosystem of free software
  • Standards: Open formats, open protocols, open languages

Recommendation: Use 100% free software. There's no excuse not to.

When Open Solutions Don't Exist: Maintain Competition and Open Standards

For gaming, where no truly open console exists:

  • Don't consolidate on Steam Deck. This would kill open graphics APIs.
  • Use Linux gaming (Bazzite, etc.) where it works well. Support open platforms when viable.
  • Use PlayStation or other consoles where Linux doesn't work. Maintain competitive pressure.
  • Result: Competition keeps Vulkan/OpenGL relevant, enabling future Linux gaming improvements.

For mobile, where no truly open phone exists:

  • Don't consolidate on GrapheneOS. This would kill open web standards.
  • Use any phone (iPhone, Android, whatever). The OS doesn't matter strategically.
  • Reject native apps entirely; use PWAs only. Promote open web standards.
  • Result: Competition keeps web standards relevant, enabling future free phones.

For AI agents, where the landscape is rapidly evolving:

  • Support open standards like MCP. Use agents that support MCP regardless of underlying model.
  • Don't consolidate on one AI provider. Competition drives open standards.
  • Use open source agents when possible. Projects like Crush benefit from MCP's existence.
  • Result: No single AI vendor controls the ecosystem; alternatives remain possible.

The Asymmetry Principle

This framework aligns with Richard Stallman's "asymmetry principle" from the GNU philosophy: encourage movement toward open standards, not toward closed ecosystems.

Approach Direction Long-term Result
Consolidate on GrapheneOS Toward Android ecosystem Android becomes permanent standard
Use any phone + PWAs only Toward open web standards Enables future free alternatives
Consolidate on Steam Deck Toward DirectX (via Proton) DirectX becomes permanent standard
Use Linux + PlayStation Toward Vulkan/OpenGL Enables future Linux gaming
Consolidate on one AI provider Toward proprietary APIs One vendor controls ecosystem
Support MCP across providers Toward open standards Enables open source agents

Tactical vs. Strategic Thinking

The key insight is distinguishing between tactical improvements and strategic direction:

GrapheneOS is a tactical improvement (better privacy than stock Android) but a strategic failure (entrenches Android ecosystem).

Using PWAs on iPhone is a tactical compromise (proprietary OS) but a strategic success (promotes open web standards).

Steam Deck is a tactical improvement (Linux-based gaming) but a strategic risk (could entrench DirectX via Proton).

Using PlayStation alongside Linux gaming is a tactical compromise (proprietary console) but a strategic success (maintains open graphics APIs).

When open solutions don't exist, strategic direction matters more than tactical purity. Your choices today determine what's possible tomorrow.

Conclusion: Think Like a Strategist

The free software movement needs more strategic thinking and less tactical purity. When truly open solutions exist, use them without compromise. But when they don't exist yet, focus on maintaining the conditions that will enable them to emerge in the future.

This means:

  • Promoting open standards over "more open" platforms
  • Maintaining competitive pressure between proprietary platforms
  • Preventing consolidation that would entrench any single vendor's APIs
  • Thinking long-term about network effects and ecosystem dynamics

Competition between proprietary platforms isn't ideal, but it's often better than consolidation around a "more open" platform that would kill the open standards we need for true alternatives to emerge.

In 2007, Steve Jobs proposed a path forward: open web standards instead of proprietary app ecosystems. We rejected it. But it's not too late.

Your behavior today shapes what's possible tomorrow. Choose strategically.
