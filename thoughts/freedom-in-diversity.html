Freedom in Diversity: A Strategic Framework for a Pluralistic FOSS Ecosystem

October 19, 2025
A diverse FOSS ecosystem, where different users make different strategic choices, is stronger and ultimately more free. This article explores how seemingly contradictory approaches can coexist and complement each other.
Disclosure and Context

I am an associate member of the Free Software Foundation and a supporter of FOSS projects. This article evolves its original thesis, arguing that a pluralistic FOSS ecosystem, where different users make different strategic choices, is stronger and ultimately more free. We will explore how seemingly contradictory approaches, such as prioritizing platform openness versus application portability, can coexist and even complement each other, leading to a more resilient and vibrant free software movement.

The Strength of Diverse Strategies

The original article presented a framework that prioritized application portability over platform openness. While this remains a valid and important strategy, it is not the only path forward. A diverse FOSS community, embracing multiple strategies, can create a more robust and adaptable ecosystem. This concept is best illustrated through real-world examples:

  • The Gaming Ecosystem: The user who chooses a Steam Deck is supporting a Linux-based platform and encouraging developers to target it. The user who chooses a Nintendo Switch but buys games that use the Vulkan API is supporting a portable, open standard. In both cases, the broader Linux gaming ecosystem benefits. The Steam Deck user pushes for platform adoption, while the Switch user pushes for open standards. These are not mutually exclusive; they are two different, valuable contributions to the same long-term goal.
  • The Mobile Ecosystem: The user who chooses an iPhone and relies on Progressive Web Apps (PWAs) is championing a future of platform-agnostic applications. The user who chooses GrapheneOS with sandboxed Android apps is pushing the boundaries of platform security and user control. Both are valid approaches to enhancing freedom in the mobile space. The PWA user reduces application lock-in, while the GrapheneOS user pioneers a more secure and private platform. The FOSS community benefits from both of these experiments.

This pluralistic approach recognizes that freedom is not a monolithic concept. Different users have different priorities, and a healthy FOSS ecosystem should accommodate a variety of freedom-enhancing strategies. The key is to understand the trade-offs of each approach and to support the development of open standards and portable applications wherever possible.

The Core Thesis Revisited: A Both/And Approach

The original thesis of this article was that “A less open platform running portable applications creates lower switching costs than a more open platform running platform-specific applications.” While this remains true, it is not the whole story. We can now reframe this as a “both/and” proposition:

A healthy FOSS ecosystem should encourage both the development of more open platforms and the adoption of portable applications. These two goals are not in opposition; they are complementary strategies that, when pursued in parallel, create a more powerful force for freedom.

This revised thesis acknowledges that platform openness and application portability are both valuable goals. The strategic question is not which one to prioritize, but how to best advance both simultaneously.

Conclusion: A More Inclusive Vision of Freedom

The FOSS community is not a monolith, and our strategies for advancing software freedom should not be either. By embracing a diversity of approaches, we can create a more resilient and adaptable ecosystem. The user who champions open platforms and the user who champions open standards are both valuable contributors to the cause of freedom. Our goal should be to foster an environment where both of these strategies can thrive, leading to a future where users have more control over their digital lives.

This is not a rejection of the original article’s arguments, but an expansion of them. The core message remains the same: your choices today shape what’s possible tomorrow. But we can now add a new layer of nuance: our collective choices, in all their diversity, are what will ultimately build the infrastructure for a free and open future.
